- Question: What is the new desalination innovation developed by UCLA engineers?
- Answer: A new class of surface-structured reverse-osmosis membranes with a polymer "brush layer" that resist clogging (scaling and fouling), are highly permeable, and can be incorporated into existing production systems.

- Question: What specific problem in reverse-osmosis desalination do these membranes address?
- Answer: They address membrane scaling and foulingâ€”build-up of salts, bacteria and colloids on the membrane surface that increase pumping energy, require costly cleaning, and shorten membrane life.

- Question: What are the three main steps used to synthesize the new membrane?
- Answer: 1) Make a polyamide thin-film composite membrane by interfacial polymerization; 2) activate the polyamide surface with atmospheric pressure plasma to create active sites; 3) perform graft polymerization from those sites with a monomer solution to grow a polymer brush layer, controlling thickness by reaction time and temperature.

- Question: How does atmospheric pressure plasma contribute and why is it advantageous?
- Answer: Atmospheric pressure plasma creates active surface sites without needing a vacuum chamber, enabling a simple, scalable "brushing" process suitable for long membranes and large-scale commercialization compared with vacuum plasma.

- Question: How does the tethered polymer brush layer prevent bacteria and colloids from attaching?
- Answer: The chemically anchored polymer chains are in constant motion (enhanced by water flow), making it difficult for organisms or particles to form stable attachments; the brush also physically screens and protects the membrane surface.

- Question: How can the membrane be engineered to repel specific charged molecules?
- Answer: By choosing the chemistry of the polymer brush layer, researchers can impart a desired surface charge so the membrane electrostatically repels molecules of the opposite charge.

- Question: What performance characteristics do the researchers report for the new membrane?
- Answer: The membrane shows high water permeability, high solute rejection, long-term stability, and superior anti-scaling properties compared to existing commercial membranes.

- Question: What are the team's planned next steps to advance the technology?
- Answer: They plan to scale the synthesis into a continuous large-format process, optimize membrane surface properties for different water sources and fouling tendencies, develop a membrane selection system, and test fouling performance under field conditions.

- Question: Who led the study and which groups were involved?
- Answer: The study's lead author is Nancy H. Lin and corresponding author is Yoram Cohen, both at the UCLA Henry Samueli School of Engineering and Applied Science, with collaboration from the UCLA Water Technology Research (WaTeR) Center and industry/water agencies.

- Question: Where and when were the findings reported?
- Answer: The findings appeared in the Journal of Materials Chemistry, with a preliminary paper in the Journal of Membrane Science the previous month; the news article reporting this was published on April 8, 2010.
