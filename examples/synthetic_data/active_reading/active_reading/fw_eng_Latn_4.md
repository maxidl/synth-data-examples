## During Reading — Specific identification attempt (thinking through the clues)
I want to match the clues to tree types. Using earlier explicit names and the clues:

- Known from text: oak, beech, sweet gum + more (two unspecified).
- Clue mapping possibilities:
  - "Good for sap & color" → Maple (sap for syrup, strong fall color)
  - "May grow nuts" → Oak or Beech (both can produce nuts; oak = acorns, beech = beechnuts). Since oak already named, this clue might point to oak or beech; but because the text named both, one clue for "may grow nuts" maps to oak and another leaf clue might indicate beech (beech has smoother, oval leaves with small teeth).
  - "3 distinct leafs" → Could point to sweet gum (star-shaped with distinct lobes) or a trifoliate species. Sweet gum usually has 5 pointed lobes, though people might describe it as having distinct points. The author explicitly named sweet gum — so "3 distinct leafs" may be a simplified kid-friendly description for the star shape (but 3 is inaccurate). Another candidate: Horse chestnut has palmate leaves (5-7 leaflets), poison ivy has 3 — but they'd avoid encouraging kids to pick poison ivy.  
  - "May grow berries" → Dogwood or holly or holly-like shrubs often have berries in fall; a "may grow berries" tree is likely dogwood (beautiful fall color too).

Reader's working hypothesis (based on text + typical local trees):
- Oak — “may grow nuts” (acorns)

- "Narrow body with pointy edges" (two items) — possibly birch and willow or two species of oak? ## After Reading — Reflection, Summary, Review, Questions, Application

1. Summary of main ideas (without looking back):  
- The author, a reporter, went into the woods to collect fallen leaves. She found many kinds and had five analyzed (oak, beech, sweet gum and two others). The page includes a cut-out activity prompting readers to go leaf-hunting (in daylight) and match five described leaves. The rest of the page lists local family events in mid-October 1999 related to nature, music, and crafts.

2. Review of annotations / key takeaways:  
- Tone: personal, playful, encourages exploration.  
- Safety: go in daylight; implied adult supervision for kids.  
- Practical: collect leaves, compare shapes and features (narrow vs. lobed vs. serrated), look for berries or nuts, note sap-producing species (maples).  
- Community tie-ins: many local programs encourage the same exploration (leaf crafts, ranger-led hikes).

3. Connections to other knowledge or experience:  
- Reminds me of school autumn nature walks and leaf identification guides. The activity can easily be adapted into a simple science lesson: classify leaves by shape, margin, venation, and fruit.

4. Questions I still have / things I'd ask the author:

- Why are two "Narrow body with pointy edges" clues repeated — are they different species or a printing error?  
- Can the author provide simple silhouette sketches next to each clue (would make ID easier)?  
- Any tips for preserving leaves for a craft (pressing, drying)?

5. Reflection on application — how I might use this information:  
- Use the cut-out checklist and go on a guided leaf-hunt with kids or a community group.

6. Quick action checklist if I were to lead this activity:
